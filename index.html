The email looked exactly right. That was the attack.

A field guide to clone phishing — the "clone sender" tactic where attackers copy a real email you've already trusted, then quietly swap the link.

Clone Sender Email Spoofing Phishing Detection
01 — Definition

What is a clone sender attack?

A clone sender attack — also called clone phishing — is a tactic where an attacker impersonates a known contact or brand by copying a legitimate, previously received email and resending a near-identical replica. Usually only the links or attachments are swapped for malicious ones, while the layout, branding, and writing style stay exactly the same.

Original
From: billing@yourvendor.com
Subject: Invoice #4471
"Your invoice is attached. Pay via the usual portal link."
Clone
From: billing@yourvend0r.com
Subject: Invoice #4471 (corrected)
"There was an error on the attached invoice — use this updated link instead."
02 — Mechanics

How clone phishing works

Interception

The attacker compromises an email account or otherwise gains access to a real piece of correspondence.

Duplication

They copy the exact layout, images, branding, and tone of a legitimate email — an invoice, a parcel tracking update, an internal IT memo.

Spoofing

They alter the From address, display name, or Reply-To address so the message looks like it came from the original sender.

The hook

The cloned message includes a pretext — "there was an error," "use this updated link" — to get the recipient to click or download without thinking twice.

03 — Detection

Red flags: how to spot a cloned email

CheckWhat to do
Header / Reply-ToInspect the actual email address, not just the display name.
Lookalike domainsWatch for subtle typos in the sender's domain, e.g. support@microsoft-update.com vs support@microsoft.com.
Hover testHover over links before clicking to preview the real destination URL.
Unexpected requestsBe suspicious of sudden requests for passwords or payment details. Verify through an independent channel.
04 — Checklist

Quick checklist

  • Does the sender's actual address match the expected domain exactly?
  • Does the link preview (on hover) match the legitimate destination?
  • Is there unexplained urgency — "error," "revised," "act now"?
  • Is sensitive information being requested unexpectedly?
  • Can the request be verified independently before acting?
05 — FAQ

Frequently asked questions

What's the difference between clone phishing and regular phishing?

Regular phishing emails are often generic. Clone phishing reuses a real, previously seen email almost exactly, which makes it harder to detect.

What's the fastest way to check if a link is safe?

Hover over the link without clicking to preview the actual destination URL, and compare it to the legitimate domain.

Should I reply to a suspicious cloned email to verify it?

No. Verify through a separate, independent channel — a phone call or a fresh message to a known-good address — not by replying to the suspicious email itself.
